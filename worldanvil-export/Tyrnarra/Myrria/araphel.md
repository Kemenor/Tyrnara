**Araphel**

**Araphel** is the god of darkness, shadows, and all things hidden — as well as all things that mortals wish to forget. In equal measure, they are also the god of rebirth and reinvention, the keeper of new names and new faces.

**Depictions**

Araphel is most commonly depicted in two forms:

- A barrel-chested **fetchling man** with waist-length hair.
- A beautiful **fetchling courtesan woman**, also with waist-length hair.

Yet no single visage is correct. Araphel is also called the **God of Many Faces**, for they wander Myrria openly in countless guises: every gender, every race, every form. The only constant is their chosen age — they always appear as someone of **thirty-five years**.

**Worship**

While rogues, thieves, and those who hide in shadows often whisper prayers to Araphel, their worship extends far beyond criminal circles.

- **Those seeking safety**: Victims fleeing danger or persecution call upon Araphel to hide them in shadow.
- **Those seeking new life**: Exiles, fugitives, or the broken-hearted pray to Araphel for a second chance, a clean slate, a new name.
- **Those seeking transformation**: Anyone who wishes to redefine themselves — to become something more than who they were — finds solace in Araphel’s blessing.

To worship Araphel is not merely to embrace shadow, but to embrace **forgetting, remaking, and becoming.**

**Role in Myrria**

Araphel’s presence defines Myrria, the **City of Second Chances**. Their temple, the **Sanctum of Veils**, is both a holy site and the resting place of the **Divine Wardstone**. While Araphel does not directly rule the city, their influence permeates it: all who come to Myrria know that here, under Araphel’s gaze, past sins may be left behind and new lives begun.

It is said Araphel walks Myrria in disguise every day, mingling with pilgrims, adventurers, and exiles alike. To meet them is to never know until afterward — only the glint of ageless eyes betrays their presence.

**Iconography of Araphel**

**Divine Symbol**

- A **half-mask** split down the center: one side smooth and unmarked, the other etched with shifting patterns. This represents both concealment and rebirth.
- Often shown **black on silver**, or **white on deep violet**, symbolizing light and darkness interwoven.

**Sacred Motifs**

- **Veil or Curtain**: Araphel’s sanctuaries are draped in silks and veils, symbolizing hidden truths and the possibility of stepping into a new self.
- **Lantern in Shadow**: A dark lantern lit with faint blue or violet fire — light enough to guide, but never to expose fully.
- **The Many Faces**: Murals and mosaics often depict Araphel in a circle of diverse faces (man, woman, elf, human, orc, etc.), all gazing inward toward a shadowed void.

**Colors**

- **Deep Violet** (rebirth, mystery)
- **Black** (shadow, forgetting)
- **Silver** (transformation, clarity within darkness)

**Holy Items**

- **Veil of the Forgotten**: Thin black cloth worn by priests during confessionals or rituals of new life.
- **Silver Masks**: Half-masks granted to initiates who dedicate themselves to Araphel’s service.
- **Shadow-Coin**: Small coins struck in blackened silver, one side blank, the other marked with the half-mask symbol. Pilgrims toss them into Myrria’s shrines when renouncing their old names.

**Gestures & Rites**

- **The Shadowed Palm**: Followers press one hand over their face, leaving only one eye uncovered — a sign of prayer and a promise to reveal only what they choose.
- **The Lantern Rite**: Lighting a lantern with dark violet flame while speaking one’s old name, then extinguishing it while declaring a new one.
