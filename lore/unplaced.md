## Settlements

### Balaena — the Skywhale City

A city built atop a young, weak skywhale that lives half-submerged in a lake. From outside it reads as an ordinary island; the people who live on Balaena do not know they are standing on a creature.

**The weight balance.** A skywhale wants to rise. The buildings and population of Balaena hold this one down. Equilibrium is fragile, so the city is governed by a strict **weight-balance protocol**: anyone entering must arrange for an equivalent weight to leave (and vice versa for anyone departing). Otherwise the island either sinks or starts to lift. **No teleporting, no landings, no take-offs** are permitted within the city's airspace.

**The bridge that walks on water.** A wooden footbridge was built years ago to connect the island to the lakeshore. Since then the whale has grown — and sunk — and the water has risen with it. The bridge now sits about five centimetres beneath the surface. To outsiders, the citizens of Balaena appear to walk across the water itself.

**The blowhole fountain.** Twice daily, the city's weight-and-pulley system temporarily presses the island down enough that the whale's blowhole erupts. The blowhole feeds an intricate fountain network throughout the city — sheets of water, rainbows in the mist, a public spectacle that draws visitors. Afterward the system is released and the island settles back. The shower also doubles as the whale's regulated breath — the people of Balaena are, without knowing it, keeping their host alive.

**The secret.** A small organisation within the city's government **knows what Balaena actually is** and works to maintain the precise balance — too weak to fly away, not so weak it dies. Their motive is the status quo: a city this beautiful, this profitable, this distinctive cannot afford for the whale to recover.

**Hooks:** weight-balance security at the gates is the city's defining ritual; the lake's mirror-still surface means anything unusual on the water is visible from far away; the secret organisation's existence is the obvious plot lever.

**Placement TBD** — needs a lake big enough and politically tractable enough. Could plausibly fit anywhere in the freshwater interior; Midarra is sea-water and ruled by pirates, so probably not there. Brauogi (Earth), Ezkudon (Knowledge), or Lautara (Commerce) are candidates.

### The Conductor and his Station

A pocket-dimension hazard on Talan's rail network.

Each train journey carries a small chance that the lights cut out, the rhythm of the rails stops, and when the windows clear the train is no longer moving through the countryside but **drifting in a starry purple void**, surrounded by floating debris: pens, suitcases, children's toys, entire orphaned train cars. The rumours call this **the Lost and Found** — a pocket dimension that catches everything left behind on every train, including, occasionally, the trains themselves.

**The Conductor** presides over the station. Tall, robed, five purple eyes; voice grating and mechanical-polite. His first words are always *"Tickets please!"* — though he is not asking about boarding tickets. Passengers must produce something else: a riddle's answer, a memory the Conductor finds interesting, a possession of personal significance. Get it right and the lights come back on and the train rolls back into ordinary tunnel-darkness. Get it wrong and you become part of the Lost and Found.

**Mechanical character:** the Lost-and-Found siding is an Abstellgleis (a side-track / parking spur) — trains that derail into the dimension are off-route, not destroyed; they can in principle be retrieved, but the Conductor keeps everything that arrives there until properly redeemed.

**Hooks:** a quest where the party must enter the Lost and Found deliberately to retrieve a specific lost item (or a person); the Conductor as a recurring strange-but-not-evil NPC; the puzzle-and-riddle format as the standard out.

**Placement TBD** — needs a placement decision. Best fits Cronus (Freedom/Travel domain), Vesuna (Chaos), or as a planar hazard tied to the Ethereal Plane / Astral Plane rather than a single domain. Author note: the original framing is whimsical-eerie, not horror. Keep that tone if developed further.

## Technology

### Grav Trains and the Ultra-High-Tech Civilisation

Talan's railways run **Grav Trains** — vehicles that are slow over open land but very fast on rail. The technology is **not original to the present age**: it was recovered, in fragmentary form, from ruins of an **Ultra-High-Tech Civilisation** that predates the current era.

Current limits:
- **Short routes only.** Long-haul rail does not exist — the recovered technology only sustains short hops.
- **Top speeds only run during Hunt.** Some seasonal / environmental factor tied to Hinka's domain (or the Hunt era of the calendar) is required for Grav Trains to reach their peak velocity. Outside that window they run at reduced speed.

**Train Pirates** are a consequence: on open land Grav Trains are slow enough to be intercepted; on rail they are fast enough to outrun any pursuit. A whole bandit specialisation has emerged around hitting the slow stretches.

**Hooks:** the Ultra-High-Tech Civilisation. Who were they? Likely candidates given current canon: a Storveldi Denbora technological branch (parallel to their integration-procedure knowledge); or a separate lost civilisation entirely; or an Elden remnant. The "during Hunt" speed constraint is also a thread — does Hinka's domain literally accelerate the trains, or is it a calendar phenomenon (an atmospheric or magical condition that only occurs in that season)?

**Placement TBD** — this is an infrastructure / world-building note, not a single location. It belongs in `world-notes.md` once the Ultra-High-Tech Civilisation question is resolved (does it become a Storveldi Denbora cousin, an Elden branch, or its own lost people?). The Train Pirates themselves probably belong in `factions.md` as a bandit category.
