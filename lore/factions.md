# Factions — Tyrnarra Lore Reference

Factions in Talan fall into four broad categories. Political entities and city-states are too numerous to list exhaustively here — see `lore/geography.md` for the full breakdown by domain.

---

## Faction Categories

**1. Political Entities** — Every kingdom, sub-region, and domain is a political faction in its own right. See `geography.md`.

**2. City-States** — The god city-states (one per bound god) plus independent city-states (Dragon's Reach, Frae City, etc.). City-states are self-governing and answer to no kingdom. See `geography.md`.

**3. God Churches** — One church per bound god (13 total). Each church operates within whatever kingdom it sits in, subject to local law, but ultimately answers to their god's theology. See `grand-gods.html` for each god's clergy structure.
- **Other God Believers** — An umbrella category for cults, churches, and traditions of non-bound gods (elven ancestral pantheons, demon lord cults, deep god worship, etc.). These have real divine power and active presence on Talan. The most politically significant instance currently is the **Divine Faith** of the Legea Empire — see entry below.

**4. Independent Organisations** — World-spanning institutions that answer to no kingdom or god. Detailed below.

## Independent Organisations

### The Adventurers Guild

**Character:** Public, open, lawful. The sword and shield of mortals.

**Origin:** Founded during the Dark Era not by kings or gods but by conscience — wanderers and soldiers with no banner who began resisting the Corrupted God because their conscience demanded it. That founding history shapes everything about the Guild. It exists because mortals needed it to exist, and it answers to that mission before any government.

**Neutrality — how it actually works:**
The Guild's defining principle is strict neutrality within the law of whatever kingdom it operates in. It holds relations with all countries and follows local law without exception in its official capacity. This means:
- If slavery is legal in a kingdom, the Guild will not post quests to free slaves — no law was broken.
- If a stolen object needs recovering, the Guild will post the quest. If the adventurers break the law in the process, the Guild will not publicly side with them if caught — but they will not mind either.
- Individual Guild members may work to overthrow unjust governments, free slaves, or undermine corrupt power — but never in official Guild capacity. The Guild has no knowledge of it officially.

This strict legalism is principled, not cowardly. It is what allows the Guild to hold relations with every kingdom simultaneously, including hostile ones. Kingdoms that despise each other both need the Guild and both know that the Guild will not be weaponised against them by their enemies.

**Scale:**
The Guild is the most geographically distributed institution on Talan.
- **Remote villages** — A single person working part-time for the Guild, sometimes managing four villages from one location.
- **Towns** — Real branch offices. A proper staff, quest boards, contracts.
- **Cities** — Guild halls. Significant infrastructure, multiple staff, storage, holding cells for contracts.
- **Capitals and city-states** — Guild Complexes. The full weight of the institution: archives, legal staff, senior officers, direct contact with Guild leadership.

**Relationship to Mercenary Guild:** Complementary and aware of each other. The strict legalism of the Adventurers Guild leaves an enormous range of work undone. The Mercenary Guild fills that gap. The two organisations are not enemies.

### The Mercenary Guild

**Character:** Shadow, coin, no loyalty beyond the contract.

**Origin:** Grew naturally from the gap left by the Adventurers Guild's strict legalism. Where the Guild cannot go officially, work still needs doing. The Mercenary Guild found those jobs.

**What they take:** Anything, if the coin is right. Kill contracts. Kidnapping. Freeing slaves in kingdoms where it is illegal. Destabilising governments. Poisoning problems that cannot be punched. The Mercenary Guild has no moral position on the work — only on payment.

**Structure:** Deliberately informal and deniable.
- No official presence in any kingdom. No legal registration, no public charter.
- Operates from the inside — sometimes literally from inside Adventurers Guild buildings, if you know who to talk to and they trust you enough to say so.
- Every kingdom knows the Mercenary Guild exists. Every kingdom knows it is dangerous to have one. Every kingdom also knows it is useful to have access to one. This mutual understanding is why it survives everywhere without being officially sanctioned anywhere.

**Scale:**
- Does not exist in remote villages.
- **Towns** — The starting point. An informal bar, a clubhouse, a back room. Nothing on paper. Nothing with a sign.
- **Cities and above** — More sophisticated networks, but still no official buildings, never a massive presence. The point is to be invisible.

**Relationship to Adventurers Guild:** Complementary. The two organisations understand each other. Mercenary Guild contacts sometimes exist within Adventurer Guild buildings — not officially, not on paper, but structurally present.

### Remnants of Corruption

**Character:** Threat, not an organisation. No unified leadership at the field level, no agenda beyond the original imperative — hunt and destroy mortal life. But the threat has *structure* above the field level: see *The Nine Generals of Corruption* below.

**Origin:** The Corrupted God was bound in 2135 MR but not destroyed. Its spawn still emerge from deep places. Certain ruins, certain mining operations that go too deep, certain sealed Elden sites — all carry risk of emergence.

**Current state:** Frequency of emergence events had dropped dramatically over four centuries — but **eight years ago (~2524 MR) the Nine Dungeons erupted**, disgorging the first great corruption armies of the modern age. The kingdoms broke that first wave; the dungeons' main entrances are now sealed. The smaller leakage continues. The Adventurers' Guild maintains a standing research operation to understand and predict emergence patterns. Some researchers believe the Corrupted God is healing; others believe the eruption was the binding's first major fracture.

**The Nine Generals of Corruption (above the leakage):** Nine corruption-lords serve as the Blightfather's hands in the mortal world, each commanding a dungeon and a legion. Unlike their master, the Nine can be slain. **One has fallen — the Ash-Binder.** Eight remain: the Vermin Queen, Rot-Tyrant, Blight-Seer, Flesh-Sculptor, Whisperer in Dreams, Maw Serpent, False Saint, Root-Twister. Full descriptions in `world-notes.md`. The Vermin Queen's lair (the Hollow of Ten Thousand Threads, Shadow Steppes) is documented in `geography.md`; the other seven dungeons' precise locations remain TBD.

**The Wardstones (counter-pressure):** Seven monumental relics anchor the chains that bind the Corrupted God. All seven sit inside Myrkono (see `world-notes.md` and `geography.md`). While they hold, the Blightfather stays bound. The fight against the Nine is also the fight to keep the Wardstones safe — this is the **War of Seals**.

**Note:** The Corrupted God is not bound by the Gods' Law — it predates it and was never party to it. The chains of the Wardstones are a separate, mortal-and-Araphel-led containment. See `world-notes.md` for full cosmology.

## Non-Bound God Faiths

### The Divine Faith (Legea Empire)

**Character:** State religion of the **Legea Empire** — a sub-region of Zuzental (Law / Forseti). A theocratic faith devoted to a god who is *not* one of the bound thirteen — a power residing in the third (Postlife) layer of the planes. Specific identity, name, and home plane (Elysium, Diyu, or Abyss) are TBD.

**Political form:** The Legea Empire is the Faith's polity. The Empire is ruled by a **demigod** of the Faith's god — half-divine, presumed scion or champion of the deity. Through this demigod, the Faith and the throne are the same institution. There is no separation of church and state in the Empire.

**Geography:** The Empire sits inside Zuzental — Forseti's domain, the domain of *law itself* — and this is the structural heart of its tension on Talan. Two divine legal authorities operate inside the same domain: Forseti's law, which is the foundation of Zuzental's continental reputation, and the Divine Faith's law, which the Empire's god claims is older and truer (the bound thirteen swore the Gods' Law; this god did not — its law has always been free). Lograth and the Thousand Kingdom uphold Forseti's framework. The Legea Empire uphold the Faith's. The boundary between them is a continual political and theological argument that the rest of Talan watches carefully.

**Reach:** Beyond the Empire proper, the Faith runs an active **missionary programme**. Most consequentially: Fenurra (see `cultures.md`). Missionaries have converted a portion of Fenurran tribes, fracturing the tribal balance. The ruling Draconis tribe has responded by arranging the political marriage of their daughter to the **theocratic prince — the heir to the Legea Empire** (and thus the heir of the demigod line) — attempting to absorb the Faith's ideology into Fenurran politics rather than let it shatter them. The outcome is unsettled.

**Why this matters cosmologically:** This is the most politically significant non-bound god currently active on Talan. Unlike demon-lord cults or deep god worship, the Divine Faith holds an entire sub-region of the continent's most prestigious domain, fields a demigod ruler, and is actively expanding. It also raises sharp questions for the Gods' Law: the bound thirteen swore the Law and reside on Talan in the Material plane; a third-layer (Postlife) god is by definition outside that compact. What it can and cannot do on Talan, and what its demigod scion's powers look like, are open. That Forseti — the literal god of binding-rules — tolerates this within his own domain is itself a question worth asking.
